# Luke Wiseman Lab — Public Profile

**PI:** Luke Wiseman
**Institution:** The Scripps Research Institute
**Department:** Department of Molecular Medicine

## Research Summary

The Wiseman lab at The Scripps Research Institute investigates how stress-responsive signaling pathways, particularly the unfolded protein response (UPR), regulate protein homeostasis across cellular compartments and how these pathways can be pharmacologically targeted to treat disease. A central focus has been elucidating the role of the ATF6 arm of the UPR in reducing the secretion and extracellular aggregation of amyloidogenic proteins such as transthyretin (TTR) and immunoglobulin light chains implicated in systemic amyloid diseases. Using quantitative interactomics and mass spectrometry-based approaches, the lab has defined molecular mechanisms by which ATF6 activation enhances ER retention of destabilized proteins through increased interactions with ER proteostasis factors including BiP and PDIA4. This work has been translated in vivo, demonstrating that pharmacologic ATF6 activation confers protection in mouse models of myocardial, renal, and cerebral ischemia/reperfusion injury. A parallel research axis addresses mitochondrial quality control, where the lab has determined cryo-EM structures of the AAA+ proteases AFG3L2 and YME1, revealed their reciprocal stress-responsive regulation, and defined how PERK signaling coordinates ER stress with mitochondrial morphology and bioenergetics. More recently, the lab has explored integrated stress response (ISR) activation through pharmacologic targeting of RIG-I and HRI, expanding their toolkit for modulating proteostasis signaling. The lab also investigates age-related changes in proteostasis using C. elegans models and develops targeted RNA sequencing platforms for medium-throughput screening of stress-pathway activators.

## Key Methods and Technologies

- Quantitative interactome proteomics (co-immunoprecipitation coupled with mass spectrometry)
- Cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) structure determination of membrane-associated protease complexes
- Targeted RNA sequencing for medium-throughput stress-responsive signaling profiling
- RNA-Seq-based transcriptional profiling and quantitative proteomics
- High-throughput screening (HTS) using cell-based luciferase reporter assays (ERSE-FLuc, XBP1s-RLuc)
- Site-directed mutagenesis and structure-function analysis of UPR signaling components
- iPSC reprogramming and differentiation for disease modeling
- Fluorogenic small-molecule probe design for live-cell and in vivo protein imaging
- Native gel electrophoresis and size-exclusion chromatography for protein oligomeric state analysis
- RNAscope in situ hybridization for tissue-level transcript localization
- Confocal and live-cell fluorescence microscopy for organelle dynamics and protein localization
- Pharmacologic compound characterization (LC-MS, metabolic activation studies, dose-response profiling)
- CRISPR/genetic knockout strategies (conditional Atf6 deletion in mouse models)
- In vivo ischemia/reperfusion injury models (myocardial, renal, cerebral in mice)
- Electron microscopy structural modeling of chaperone complexes
- Calcium imaging and mitochondrial bioenergetics assays

## Model Systems

- HEK293T and HEK293T-Rex cells with stably integrated UPR reporter constructs
- HEK293 DAX cells for ATF6 activation studies
- HepG2 hepatocyte cells and HepG2 ATF6 reporter cells
- ATF6+/+ and ATF6-/- mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs)
- Cardiac myocyte-specific Atf6 conditional knockout mice
- IRE1α conditional knockout mice (liver-specific)
- Mouse models of myocardial, renal, and cerebral ischemia/reperfusion injury
- C. elegans models for age-related mitochondrial proteome studies
- C. elegans expressing human transthyretin transgenically
- ATTR amyloidosis patient-derived induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs)
- FLAG-tagged TTR variant expression systems (wild-type and destabilized mutants)
- Destabilized immunoglobulin light chain expression systems for AL amyloidosis modeling
- Reconstituted AAA+ protease complexes (AFG3L2, YME1) for cryo-EM structural studies
- Non-obese diabetic (NOD) and C57BL/6 mouse embryonic stem cells

## Disease Areas / Biological Processes

- Systemic amyloid diseases (TTR amyloidosis, light chain amyloidosis)
- Ischemia/reperfusion injury (myocardial infarction, stroke, renal ischemia)
- Neurodegenerative diseases (spinocerebellar ataxia, AFG3L2-linked neurodegeneration)
- Achromatopsia (ATF6 loss-of-function retinal disease)
- ER stress-related protein misfolding diseases
- Mitochondrial dysfunction and mitochondrial quality control disorders
- Age-related decline in proteostasis capacity
