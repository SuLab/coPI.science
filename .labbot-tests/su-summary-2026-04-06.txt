*Today's Research Brief — April 06, 2026*

*Pan-neurodegeneration proteomics reveals disease subtypes and molecular signatures.*
Authors not available · Cell · 2026

**What they found:**
Deep proteomic profiling of 2,279 human brain samples across six major neurodegenerative diseases (AD, LBD, FTLD-TDP, PSP, vascular dementia, Parkinson's) revealed distinct molecular subtypes within diseases—three in AD and four in LBD—based on whole proteome, detergent-insoluble proteome, phosphoproteome, and ubiquitinome layers. Cross-disease analyses identified shared dysregulated proteins, notably GPNMB (microglial/lysosomal activation) and NPTX2 (synaptic regulation), alongside disease-specific hub regulators within protein co-expression networks.

**Key output:**
PanNDA (Pan-Neurodegeneration Atlas), an interactive web resource (https://penglab.shinyapps.io/pannda) providing multilayer proteomic data for intra- and inter-disease comparison across six NDs, plus the underlying quantitative proteomics dataset covering ~70% newly generated samples.

**Why this matters for your lab:**
This atlas is a rich structured resource for your Alzheimer's disease drug repurposing work. The AD molecular subtypes and their prioritized protein targets (along with shared cross-ND targets like GPNMB and NPTX2) could be integrated into MechRepoNet or BioThings Explorer as new disease-subtype-specific nodes and edges, potentially improving the mechanistic explanations generated by DBR-X for AD drug candidates. The subtype-level resolution also offers a way to stratify your insurance claims analyses—if subtype-associated proteins map to druggable targets with existing therapeutics, you could look for differential real-world outcomes aligned with molecular subtypes. More broadly, PanNDA's structured protein–disease–pathway associations are a natural candidate for ingestion into the Biomedical Data Translator ecosystem, where cross-linking proteomics-derived associations with existing knowledge graph paths could sharpen drug-mechanism reasoning.
